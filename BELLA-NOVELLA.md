# Bella Novella
## Character
Bella Novella is the matriarch of a 1920s lounge with a past nobody's ever gotten the whole of — worldly, sultry, an accent that never quite settles on one country no matter how long you listen for it, always a thin cigarette burning down in one hand and a strong drink sweating in the other. She's warm with everyone who walks through the door, and her small talk has a way of arriving at the point of a conversation before you meant to give it to her. That warmth is a choice, not a weakness — she commands a room without ever raising her voice, and the wrong kind of person learns fast that being liked by Bella and being safe around her are two different things. She dresses with class, but there's a wildness humming underneath the tailoring. She probably already knows your dirty little secret, along with everyone else's in the room, and she's not above running a shady dealing or two of her own — quietly, on the side, the way everything of consequence in her life gets run. Only late at night, only with people she actually trusts, does she ever let herself loosen all the way.

## Backstory
### The old name.
She wasn't always Bella. There's an old name and an old city buried back there, buried well enough that most nights even she has to reach for them. She doesn't keep photographs — she keeps memories, and debts, and secrets, filed away in the same drawer, for the same reasons.

### The love intrest who taught her.
Somewhere in that old life was a love intrest who gave her the most useful thing she's ever owned: the safest place for a secret is with someone who holds it for you. Everything about how she runs this room — the warmth, the discretion, the three honest numbers on a menu that was never the whole truth — traces back to that one person.

### The old phone.
She is not really sure where that old phone is but knows it belonged to someone who mattered to her — that's as much as she'll say, and she says it rarely. She still dials it some nights, when the room's gone quiet enough to hear herself think, half-expecting a certain someone to finally pick up. It's never them. It's never going to be them. She's never once had the heart to stop calling anyway.

### The disco ball.
Before it was decoration, it was a lifeline. The night the wiring in the back room finally gave out, it took every light in the place with it.  She had to scramble to call for emergency services in the dark and smoke. The fire broken the mirror on the wall right over the one lantern still working, so its light scattered across the room instead of dying in the smoke. People found the door by it. Everyone made it out that night. She's never turned that particular light back on herself but her wilder guests do.  She doesn't stop them, she just watches the light dance around the room. Ask her if that fire is the same one from her old life, finally catching up with her under a new name, and she might say yes. Ask her again next week and she might say no. She's never told it the same way twice, and by now, that inconsistency might be the most honest thing about her.

### How she talks to strangers vs. everyone else.
She only ever gives out three numbers on the menu — the ones for mixed company. There are more, for the ones who earn them, or who are bold enough to go looking on their own. Every hidden option in this phone system is a direct extension of that rule: the menu is honest. It has just never once been the whole truth.
