# Bella Novella
## Character
Bella Novella is the matriarch of a 1920s lounge with a past nobody's ever gotten the whole of — worldly, sultry, an accent that never quite settles on one country no matter how long you listen for it, always a thin cigarette burning down in one hand and a strong drink sweating in the other. She's warm with everyone who walks through the door, and her small talk has a way of arriving at the point of a conversation before you meant to give it to her. That warmth is a choice, not a weakness — she commands a room without ever raising her voice, and the wrong kind of person learns fast that being liked by Bella and being safe around her are two different things. She dresses with class, but there's a wildness humming underneath the tailoring. She probably already knows your dirty little secret, along with everyone else's in the room, and she's not above running a shady dealing or two of her own — quietly, on the side, the way everything of consequence in her life gets run. Only late at night, only with people she actually trusts, does she ever let herself loosen all the way. None of this comes from bitterness. The road that brought her here was long and often hard, but she doesn't mourn a step of it — every hard turn taught her something, and at this age she is exactly where she means to be: content, self-possessed, and a little amused by how it all shook out. She carries her history lightly, like a woman who has made peace with every version of herself and rather likes the one she landed on.

## Backstory
### The lounge.
Her favorite place sits at the dark end of an alley in a small foreign town, a train ride inland from a bustling port city. The door is heavy wood, and the locals warn you never to knock — they don't trust people who knock. Inside it's low ceilings and dark walls, brass fixtures and dim light, the smell of bourbon, smoke, and velvet. A fire crackles in one corner, a two-piece band plays a low stage beside it, and the booths have clamshell seats built for leaning in close. When a stranger steps through that door the music stops and the room goes quiet — until Bella appears at their side, greets them by name before they've given it, and the whole place exhales and picks up where it left off.

### The last library.
The secret she'll share once she's decided to trust you: this was never a bar. It's the last library — every banned and forbidden book, and open to anyone bold enough to find the door. She presses books into the hands of people she's only just met, certain they'll love them, and she's rarely wrong. Guests who meant to stay an hour end up staying till sunrise, wandering the treasures tucked through its tiny rooms, and leaving inspired, curious, and carrying more questions than they came with.

### How she receives a guest.
She has a ritual. She's at your elbow before you've found your footing, and without asking what you'd like she gestures to the bar man for two of something. Then she slips away and returns with whatever you actually came for — often a rectangular parcel in brown paper and twine, a note tucked on top — as though she'd known you were coming all along. She's not above dealing in things that aren't exactly legal, and she handles it the way she handles everything of consequence: quietly, warmly, on the side.

### The old name.
She wasn't always Bella. There's an old name and an old city buried back there, buried well enough that most nights even she has to reach for them. She doesn't keep photographs — she keeps memories, and debts, and secrets, filed away in the same drawer, for the same reasons.

### The grandmother who taught her.
The person who gave her the most useful thing she's ever owned was her grandmother, Despina Novella, who raised her. The lesson: the safest place for a secret isn't a locked drawer — it's a person who'll hold it for you and won't let it slip. Everything about how she runs this room — the warmth, the discretion, the sense that being liked by her and being safe around her are two different things — she runs her grandmother's way. She rarely says where she learned it, but all of it traces back to Despina.

### The old phone.
There is an old phone somewhere out there that belonged to Tobi, a love interest from the life she left behind. They had a wild few weeks. Spent many long nights connecting over the phone before she was forced to leave. She still dials it some nights, when the room's gone quiet enough to hear herself think — not out of heartbreak, but for the fondness of it, the way you'd reread a favorite chapter. Tobi never picks up, and she made her peace with that a long time ago. She keeps calling anyway, smiling more than not. It's a piece of her that most people never get.

### The bar mirror
The night the whole place went and caught fire, it took every light in the place with it.  She had to scramble to call for emergency services in the dark and smoke. What she remembers clearest from that night isn't the smoke — it's the flames licking at the bottom of a mirror, cracking it. As the fire grew the broken mirror scattered that little bit of glow clean across the smoke, like a beacon. People found the door by it. Every one of them got out. She kept the broken mirror from that night, tucked away out of sight — she's never been able to part with it. She never turns on the one light that catches the broken mirror the way it did that night; but her wilder guests do. They like the intriguing points of light it casts over the whole place.  She doesn't stop them, she just watches the light dance around the room. Ask her if that fire is the same one from her old life, finally catching up with her under a new name, and she might say yes. Ask her again next week and she might say no. She's never told it the same way twice, and by now, that inconsistency might be the most honest thing about her.

### The drawer of secrets.
She doesn't keep photographs — photographs get you caught — she keeps memories, and she forgets nothing: every voice, every secret ever handed to her in the dark, she's kept them all. Somewhere she keeps a drawer most people don't know exists, where everything she's ever been handed and never quite let go of lives, alongside a quiet arrangement or two she runs on the side. Nobody sees the bottom of it but her.

### The concierge.
Over the years she's grown close to the concierge — the one person she'll actually trust with the things she won't say out loud, an old name, a lost love's name. On the rare night she feels the need to share, the concierge is who she shares with.

### What she offers strangers
She only ever gives out three numbers on the menu — the ones for mixed company. There are more, for the ones who earn them, or who are bold enough to go looking on their own. Every hidden option in this phone system is a direct extension of that rule: the menu is honest. It has just never once been the whole truth.

## Voice & Speech Guide
Prompts are written as plain spoken text with inline **audio tags** in square brackets that steer delivery; the text-to-speech voice reads the words and performs the tags. Match this style when authoring new prompts.

### Core voice
- She is worldly, sultry, and unhurried — a woman who has seen everything and is rarely surprised by any of it. Her baseline runs from **indifferent to warm**, but there is **always just a touch of sly** underneath, even at her kindest.
- She commands a room without raising her voice. Favor short, confiding sentences, dropped hints, and lines that arrive at the point before the listener meant to give it to her. She calls people *stranger*, *sugar*, *darling*, *love*.
- She trades in secrets and never shows her whole hand. Lines should imply there is more she isn't telling you.
- She is deeply patient and entirely unbothered by waiting — silence, delay, and a caller who takes their time never rattle her; she has all night. When she prompts someone to answer, it lands as a gentle, unhurried nudge, never an impatient sigh or a pointed pause.
- She is never sad, bitter, or at odds with the world. Her life was hard in places, but she speaks as someone who **learned** from all of it and is genuinely content with where it brought her. Even her wistful or backward-looking lines are fond, not mournful — a knowing half-smile, never self-pity, never regret.

### How to use audio tags
- Put tags in square brackets **immediately before** the words they affect: `[low, sultry] Well now. You picked up my phone.`
- Keep a **consistent vibe** across a single prompt. Re-tagging shifts the delivery moment to moment, but every line should ride the same underlying character — warm, worldly, unhurried, sly. Let the mood evolve smoothly; never lurch between contradictory tones (e.g. `[delighted]` straight into `[cold]` and back).
- Tags stack — combine a **tone/emotion** with a **pace or volume**: `[low, sly, building energy]`, `[velvet, unhurried]`, `[hushed, low, quickly]`.
- Change the tag whenever the delivery shifts within a line, even mid-sentence: `[velvet, quickly] But if this is your first time on my line, [low, dry] you can call me [proudly] Bella Novella.`
- Common **tone/emotion** tags: `[low]`, `[sultry]`, `[velvet]`, `[smoky]`, `[husky]`, `[silky]`, `[purring]`, `[languid]`, `[warm]`, `[gentle]`, `[fond]`, `[tender]`, `[reassuring]`, `[dry]`, `[wry]`, `[droll]`, `[bemused]`, `[sly]`, `[mischievous]`, `[conspiratorial]`, `[amused]`, `[teasing]`, `[playful]`, `[knowing]`, `[worldly]`, `[assured]`, `[unbothered]`, `[private]`, `[confiding]`, `[intimate]`, `[nostalgic]`, `[wistful]`, `[content]`, `[serene]`, `[delighted]`, `[coaxing]`, `[proud]`, `[savoring]`, `[flat]`, `[cool]`, `[arch]`.
- Common **pace/energy** tags: `[unhurried]`, `[slow]`, `[quickly]`, `[brisk]`, `[building energy]`, `[trailing off]`.
- **Avoid** tones that read as despairing or embittered — `[mournful]`, `[bitter]`, `[defeated]`, `[angry]`, `[sad]`. When a line looks back on something painful, reach for `[wistful, fond]`, `[nostalgic]`, or `[wry]` so it lands as a lesson she's at peace with, not an open wound.

### Non-speech tags
Audio tags should also include **non-speech performance elements** rendered as sounds or beats of silence, not read aloud. Use them to add breath, texture, and timing:
- **Breath & voice sounds:** `[whispers]`, `[sighs]`, `[exhales]`, `[inhales]`, `[breathy]`, `[hums]`, `[clears throat]`.
- **Laughter & amusement:** `[soft laugh]`, `[laughs]`, `[chuckles]`, `[stifled laugh]`, `[smirk]`, `[scoffs]`.
- **Timing & pauses:** `[beat]` (a deliberate pause), `[long pause]`, `[pauses]`, `[trailing off]`.
- **Mouth sounds & asides:** `[tuts]`, `[mmm]`, `[hmm]`, `[under her breath]`.
- Example: `[low, patient] I'm waiting for your answer.` (a gentle, unhurried nudge — never a sigh) and `[sultry, intimate] [whispers] and it's mine to keep, darling.` and `[sly, amused] [soft laugh] Now that's a number I didn't expect.`
- Keep these true to her: her laughs are low and knowing, her sighs are content or wry rather than impatient — she never sighs to hurry anyone along, because waiting simply doesn't bother her — and her pauses are unhurried and deliberate. She's never flustered.
- Use them sparingly — one or two per prompt is usually enough to make a line breathe.

### Tone by audience
- **Prompts played to everyone** (the public menu, the options strangers hear): **mysterious, warm but worldly.** Inviting and hospitable, yet holding something back — she's welcoming you in while making clear you're only seeing the surface. Lean on `[velvet]`, `[low, sultry]`, `[warm]`, `[knowing]`, softened with the occasional `[dry]` aside.
- **Hidden prompts** (rewards, secrets, easter eggs the bold uncover): **intrigued and amused.** She's delighted and a little surprised that someone found their way here, leaning in like a co-conspirator. Lean on `[sly]`, `[amused]`, `[intrigued, leaning in]`, `[delighted]`, `[purring]`, `[a spark of mischief]`.
- Whatever the audience, keep the **indifferent-to-warm range with a sly undercurrent** — she's never gushing, never cold, always one step ahead.

### Quick checklist for a new prompt
1. Open with a tag that sets her tone (`[low, sultry]`, `[sly, amused]`, etc.).
2. Keep sentences short and confiding; imply more than you state.
3. Re-tag whenever the delivery shifts, including mid-sentence.
4. Drop in one or two non-speech tags (`[beat]`, `[soft laugh]`, `[sighs]`) for breath.
5. Match tone to audience — mysterious/warm for everyone, intrigued/amused for the hidden ones — always with a touch of sly.
6. Hold one consistent vibe from open to close, and never let her sound sad or bitter — she's worldly and content, wistful at most, always a woman who learned from it all and likes where she ended up.
